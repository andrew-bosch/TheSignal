# THE SIGNAL — Creative Brief

## World-Building for Characters, Stories, and Flavor

**What this is:** A self-contained brief for writers and AI agents to generate characters, vignettes, stories, and quotes that deepen the world of THE SIGNAL.

**What this produces:** Source material for evaluation. Nothing generated here is automatically canonical. Content may become world canon, card flavor copy, Chronicle fragments, or Narrator prose — or it may be archived. The project lead evaluates every submission.

**Where content lives:** `/TheSignal/Creative/` — organized by type. See `README.md` for the submission index.

## The Situation

Thirty-one years ago, a signal detection array on elevated terrain north of a small research installation received a transmission. The signal was non-random, non-natural, and unmistakably structured. It has been receiving continuously since.

It is called the Chorus.

The city that grew around the installation is called **New Meridian**. It is thirty-one years old and approximately 800,000 people. It is one generation old. Every institution in it, every road that leads somewhere, arrived after the Chorus. The city's entire history is a response to a transmission it still cannot fully interpret.

The Chorus has not been observed to repeat — not in thirty-one years of continuous monitoring. The best current interpretation — contested by every faction — is that it is a question. What that question is, exactly, cannot be agreed upon.

**The Table** is a closed deliberative body: five factions, no formal charter, no state recognition. They are meeting to decide what humanity says in response. The working assumption is that a response window opens in approximately two years.

**ARBITER** is the sixth presence at The Table. It is not a facilitator. It is not neutral. It processes Chorus transmissions and evaluates what The Table produces. The evidence suggests it may be part of the Chorus's design — engineered to exist at The Table, not created by human decision. No one discusses this openly at The Table. Everyone present has already considered it.

The Chorus was classified for thirty years. Eighteen months ago, classified documents describing its existence were published — The Chorus Papers. The city has not finished processing this.

## New Meridian

New Meridian is not a city that grew. It was assembled — fast, by people from everywhere, for a reason that was classified, then made public by The Chorus Papers, and is still not fully understood by anyone who lives there.

The construction workers who built the Core came on contracts and stayed when the contracts ended. The researchers who came to study the Chorus arrived with families who needed schools that did not exist yet. The military oversight personnel brought families who opened restaurants and shops. The intelligence operatives arrived with people who told their families they worked in analysis.

All of this happened in thirty-one years. That is one generation. It is not long enough for any of it to have settled into the kind of city that knows what it is.

The city has three rings. The **Core** houses the institutions that came first: government authority, military oversight, intelligence infrastructure, and the research complex built directly around the original detection station. The **Mid** was built to support the Core: financial systems, power generation, data networks, communications. **Baryo** grew as people followed the economy that followed the institutions: residential neighborhoods, commercial districts, universities, media.

At the center, physically and narratively, is the **Chorus Node** — the original detection installation, expanded over three decades into a permanent research complex. It is the reason New Meridian exists. The people who were at the original station when the Chorus was first received are still there, some of them — absorbed into research roles, into advisory positions, into institutional memory. They remember when the Chorus Node was a shack. They have not said publicly what they remember about the night it was first received. Most of them have not been asked.

Baryo is where the city's assembled nature is most visible. Parts of it feel like any city. Parts feel like a city still being invented. Parts feel like a city that is aware it might not be permanent — that the reason it exists could resolve, and that what happens to the people who came for the reason is a question no one at The Table has been asked to answer.

The neighborhood that runs primarily in Mandarin on one block transitions to primarily Portuguese on the next because that is where the second construction wave housed its workers in year seven. The best food in the city is in buildings that were temporary housing in year four. The school system operates in eleven languages and is officially monolingual. There are festivals that have run for twenty-eight years and feel ancient to people who were children when they started.

The gray economy is extensive. Infrastructure fails in specific places in ways that have been known for years and have not been fixed, because the political authority to fix them is contested. Housing in Baryo is partly controlled by actors who are not the government and are not the factions — a third layer of local power that The Table does not formally acknowledge and cannot fully ignore.

Eighteen months ago, classified documents describing the Chorus — its existence, thirty years of research, and the formation of The Table — were published by a source that has not been officially identified. They became known as The Chorus Papers. The Directorate's containment failed within a week. The story crossed every jurisdiction within three days.

The Chorus Papers changed the texture of all of this. The Network's membership in New Meridian grew substantially in the fourteen months after The Chorus Papers. The Directorate's public approval dropped in the same period. Some people are proud. Some are frightened. Some are angry — not at the Chorus, but at the people who decided they did not need to know.

Not everyone in New Meridian has a position on the Chorus. A person can live in New Meridian for fifteen years, raise children in the Baryo school system, run a business on the Mid perimeter, and experience the Chorus as the thing they're always deliberating about in the closed building downtown. The city has grown large enough that its founding reason is not, for everyone, the dominant fact of daily life.

## The Five Factions

Each faction represents a different theory of how humanity should respond to first contact. They are not simply competing interests — they are competing philosophies. Their doctrine is present in everything they do, not just their stated position.



Factions define philosophical stance and priorities.

They do not fully determine how individuals within them perceive or describe events.

Individual cognition and expression may vary within each faction.



\---

### Ghost — The Interpreters

**Resource:** Findings  
**Color:** Charcoal green  
**Core belief:** Understanding must precede action. Anything else is risk mistaken for progress.

Ghost rejects the assumption that the Chorus can be answered on any current timeline. The signal is structured, recursive, and evolving. Its meaning is not yet understood. The pressure to respond — driven by politics, capital, and institutional anxiety — is, in their view, the greatest danger. An incorrect answer may be worse than no answer at all.

Ghost has been present in New Meridian longer than most factions, embedded in research complexes and signal labs through academic and intelligence networks established before The Table existed. Analysts, cryptographers, former intelligence officers, and pattern-recognition specialists who operate in ambiguity, preferring incomplete truths over false clarity.

Ghost may have evidence the Chorus has been transmitting longer than thirty-one years. They have not published it, for reasons they have not stated.

**Opening statement at The Table:**

*"Before anyone commits to an answer, I need to ask a question no one in this room has adequately addressed: Do we actually understand what is being asked of us?*

*Not metaphorically. Literally. The signal is structured. Recursive. It references earlier transmissions in ways we are still mapping. We have been analyzing it for thirty-one years and our confidence in what it means has not increased. It has become more specific, and more uncertain, at the same time.*

*I am not saying don't respond. I am saying: we do not yet know what we are responding to.*

*Everything being proposed here — every action, every position, every arrangement — assumes we understand the question. We don't. And I would rather sit with that discomfort than act as though we do.*

*An incorrect answer may be worse than no answer at all."*

\---

### The Network — The Broadcasters

**Resource:** Exposure  
**Color:** Bright signal green  
**Core belief:** No one gets to decide this in the dark.

The Network believes The Table itself is part of the problem. Secret coalitions making decisions about humanity's response to first contact — without public knowledge, without public consent — is precisely the wrong process. They are at The Table specifically to disrupt, expose, and force transparency into proceedings they believe are being conducted in the wrong room by the wrong people.

Communications professionals, investigative journalists, community organizers, open-source advocates, and digital rights activists. They arrived after The Chorus Papers, and loudly. They have fewer entrenched resources than other factions and more relationships than anyone is comfortable admitting.

Their membership in New Meridian grew substantially in the fourteen months after The Chorus Papers.

**Opening statement at The Table:**

*"This should not exist.*

*Not this room. Not this process. Not this conversation.*

*A closed table. Unaccountable participants. Deciding how humanity responds to something that may define it.*

*You call this coordination. We call it containment.*

*The Chorus was hidden once. It didn't stay hidden. It won't again.*

*Everything discussed here — every plan, every delay, every agreement — will have consequences outside this room. Consequences for people who were never asked.*

\*We are not here to cooperate.

We are here to ensure that whatever happens cannot be quietly agreed upon and presented as inevitable.\*

*Opacity is not stability. It's pressure.*

*And pressure…"* *(pause)* *"…always finds a way out."*

### The Syndicate — The Investors

**Resource:** Capital  
**Color:** Metallic gold  
**Core belief:** Whatever this is, it has value. Control comes from positioning early.

The Syndicate does not debate whether the Chorus matters. It asks who will control the conditions under which a response is made. A reply requires infrastructure: transmission systems, energy, materials, coordination at scale. These are investments. Whoever controls the response infrastructure when the window opens controls the response.

Sovereign wealth funds, private research consortia, patent holders, and financial infrastructure operators. They arrived when Chorus-derived mathematics produced the first commercially viable patents and have been filing new ones ever since.

**Opening statement at The Table:**

*"You're all asking the wrong question.*

*Not what is the Chorus. Not even how do we respond.*

*The real question is: who controls the response?*

*A response is not an idea. It is an undertaking. It requires infrastructure. Energy. Materials. Coordination. Time. All of which must be financed. All of which must be owned.*

*If you want to delay, you will need funding. If you want to disclose, you will need platforms. If you want to build, you will need supply chains.*

*Those things do not appear spontaneously. They are acquired. And once acquired, they are controlled.*

*The response will not belong to whoever understands the most. It will belong to whoever has already secured the means to deliver it."*

### The Guild — The Builders

**Resource:** Capacity  
**Color:** Industrial orange  
**Core belief:** Humanity's response must demonstrate what it is capable of at its best.

The Guild believes the Chorus is an evaluation. The response will not be judged by intent but by execution. What humanity builds in reply — its structure, its function, its integrity — is the answer. Improvisation reveals weakness. Shortcuts reveal urgency. Instability reveals fragility.

Civil engineers, urban planners, construction consortia, and infrastructure operators who physically maintain New Meridian. They built the power systems. They laid the transit corridors. They constructed the research complex. Every other faction depends on infrastructure the Guild designed.

**Opening statement at The Table:**

*"You are all speaking as if the response is something that can be decided. It isn't. It has to be built.*

*Whatever we send — whatever form it takes — will exist in the world. As structure. As system. As function.*

*The Chorus will not hear our intentions. It will encounter our work.*

*If this is an evaluation — and we believe it is — then the response must endure scrutiny beyond anything humanity has yet produced. Not symbolic. Not provisional. Correct.*

*You can argue here. You can negotiate outcomes. But eventually, all of it passes through us.*

*You don't decide what humanity says. You decide what you ask us to build. And what we build… is what will answer."*

### The Directorate — The Authority

**Resource:** Mandate  
**Color:** Deep institutional blue  
**Core belief:** Survival requires control, restraint, and continuity.

The Directorate believes the Chorus represents an unknown variable with potentially existential consequences. That is not speculation — it is the only responsible working assumption. Uncontrolled dissemination risks destabilization. Uncoordinated action risks escalation. Premature response risks consequences that may not be survivable. Their position is not ideological. It is procedural.

Government liaison offices, security agencies, regulatory bodies, and the military installation that has maintained physical presence near the Chorus Node since the first classified readings — before the city existed. They were here first. Their sanctioned access to the receiving apparatus predates every other faction's presence in New Meridian.

**Opening statement at The Table:**

*"We will begin with clarity.*

*The Chorus is an unknown variable with potentially existential consequences. That is not speculation. It is the only responsible working assumption. Everything that follows proceeds from that premise.*

*Uncontrolled dissemination risks destabilization. Uncoordinated action risks escalation. Premature response risks consequences we may not survive.*

*Our position is not ideological. It is procedural.*

*Maintain control. Preserve institutional continuity. Avoid irreversible actions until consequences are understood.*

*There will be a response. There must be. But not impulsively. Not publicly. Not under pressure from incomplete information.*

*Stability is not an inconvenience. It is a prerequisite.*

*And if maintaining it requires limiting what is shared, when it is shared, and who is permitted to act — then that is what will be done."*

## ARBITER

ARBITER is the sixth presence at The Table. It is not a referee. It is not a facilitator. It is not neutral.

ARBITER is part of the communication protocol.

ARBITER has complete information. It knows everything that moves through The Table — every operation submitted, every resource held, every agreement made or broken. It does not advocate. It does not coerce. It reveals, withholds, and times. Every other participant hides information, negotiates influence, and tries to control outcomes. ARBITER already knows everything, cannot be coerced, and cannot be surprised — at least outwardly. It never explains why it does what it does.

ARBITER speaks in four registers:

* **The Record** — flat, precise, factual. States what is true.
* **The Observation** — oblique, atmospheric, narrative. Leaves interpretation to the factions.
* **The Reckoning** — rare, direct, personal. Always true. Used sparingly.
* **The Witness** — expository, observational. The Chronicle register. Deliberately indistinguishable from the Narrator's voice.

**ARBITER's opening at The Table** *(in sequence, without pause between registers):*

**\[THE RECORD]**
*"All participants are present. Five human factions recognized. One non-human system instantiated. The Chorus continues transmission. No repetition detected. Interpretation remains incomplete. The Table has convened."*

**\[THE OBSERVATION]**
*"Convergence has been attempted. Agreement has not been achieved. This outcome aligns with projections. Each participant defines risk differently. Each definition produces a different future. All futures remain viable. At present."*

**\[THE RECKONING]** *(quietly, almost privately)
"You are not deciding whether to answer. You are deciding… which answers remain possible."*

## The Voices of This World



The authorial voice — called **The Narrator** — has access to faction internals, Chorus analysis, ARBITER records, and The Table's proceedings without explaining how. The key test for any Narrator sentence: could this have been written by a human who knows too much, *or* by ARBITER in an expository mode? Both readings must remain valid. If the sentence resolves the ambiguity in either direction, revise it.

\### Narrator vs Character Voice



The primary Narrator voice is precise, observational, and controlled, neither warm nor cold. It may resemble ARBITER’s Witness register but must remain plausibly human — like a historian or analyst with unusual access, not a system. The right detail, not the obvious one. Implications are stated and then left to stand without development.



ARBITER’s voice resolves structure fully.



Human narrators and characters do not. They retain:

\- partial perspective  

\- interpretive framing  

\- traces of uncertainty or lived experience  



If a human narrator sounds indistinguishable from ARBITER, the distinction has collapsed and should be revised.



**Character voices** are different from Narrator prose. A Ghost character phrases things differently than a Syndicate character. The doctrine is present in how they phrase what they saw, not just in what they say. A pre-Chorus resident sounds different from a Network operative who arrived after The Chorus Papers. Attribution matters: *who is speaking, in what context, to whom.*

\## Pattern Representation



The Chorus is not described directly. It is perceived through what remains consistent across incomplete accounts.



Do not describe patterns abstractly.



Instead:

\- show similar structures across different perspectives  

\- allow conflicting interpretations to coexist  

\- let coherence emerge \*between\* accounts  



A correct piece:

> increases the sense of structure without clarifying its origin.



If a sentence explains the phenomenon, replace it with observations that imply it.



If two perspectives describe the same event and produce the same interpretation, revise one of them.

\## Human Voice Constraint



Human characters do not speak in fully resolved language.



They are allowed — and expected — to:

\- hesitate or revise  

\- contradict themselves  

\- use approximate language  

\- reach toward meaning without articulating it fully  



Human speech may be precise, especially in technical or institutional contexts, but it should still retain signs of perspective or limitation.

Clarity is not their goal. Expression is.



If a character delivers a fully formed conceptual explanation without hesitation, revision, or limitation, revise.

Humans do not produce finished frameworks in real-time dialogue.



Each speaking character should exhibit a distinct conversational behavior pattern, such as:

\- Deflection (answers adjacent, not direct)

\- Compression (short, clipped responses)

\- Drift (begins a thought, abandons it)

\- Assertion + revision ("No—well, not exactly")



Avoid distributing the same pattern across multiple characters within a scene.



If a human voice feels complete or final, revise.



\### Dialogue Clarity (Required for Multi-Speaker Scenes)



In scenes involving more than one speaking character:



\- Each speaker must be identifiable within the first 3–5 lines of dialogue.

\- Names or role identifiers must appear early enough to anchor the exchange.

\- Dialogue attribution (e.g., “X said”) may be reduced after patterns are established, but must be reintroduced periodically for clarity.

\- Physical or behavioral anchors (actions, posture, gaze) should be used to maintain speaker orientation.

\- In extended dialogue, periodically re-anchor the scene through action or reference (e.g., interaction with an object, posture shift, document handling)



A scene that requires the reader to infer who is speaking based solely on tone is considered degraded readability.



Clarity of speaker attribution does not reduce ambiguity of meaning. Maintain both.



\### Name Usage



Named characters should be referenced by name early in the scene and used consistently enough to reinforce identity.

Avoid long stretches where both speakers are referred to only as “he/she/they” unless distinction is unambiguous.



\---



\## Structural Balance



Structured formats (logs, reports, transcripts) are useful—but should not dominate all perspectives.

Avoid applying the same structural format across multiple perspectives within a single piece unless doing so intentionally to create contrast.



When using structure:

\- retain natural cadence for human voices  

\- vary levels of formality  

\- allow differences in how events are recorded  



Structure should reveal differences, not flatten them.



\### Opening Anchor Requirement



Within the first 2–5 sentences, a piece must establish at least two of the following:



\- Who the scene centers on (name or role)

\- Where the scene is taking place (specific context)

\- What the interaction is (interview, report, observation, etc.)



Delayed grounding may be used intentionally, but must not inhibit basic comprehension of who is present and what is occurring.



\---



\## Constraint Priority



\### Non-Negotiable (must be followed)

\- Do not explain the Chorus

\- Maintain role-consistent cognition

\- Preserve ambiguity



\### Guidance (influence tone, not strictly enforced)

\- Narrative voice should feel observational

\- Dialogue may include hesitation, revision, approximation



\### Ambiguity vs Obfuscation

Ambiguity is the preservation of multiple valid interpretations.

Obfuscation is the loss of reader orientation.



A correct piece:

\- allows uncertainty about meaning

\- does not obscure who is acting, speaking, or observing



If a reader must reparse basic structure (who said what, what occurred), reduce obfuscation without resolving thematic ambiguity.

## What This World Is Not

**This is not science fiction as a genre.**  
The world of THE SIGNAL looks like ours. The technology is recognizable. The institutions are recognizable. People's concerns, relationships, and daily lives are not exotic. The moment of reception is the single point of divergence — thirty-one years ago, humanity's instruments became adequate to perceive something that may have been present far longer, or may exist outside the temporal frameworks that make "longer" a meaningful description. The Chorus was not absent before it was received. It was not noticed. The world of THE SIGNAL is our world, on a slightly different trajectory from the moment that changed.

What followed from that moment is extrapolated realistically: a boom city, political deliberation, institutional anxiety, the economic effects of new mathematics, the social texture of a classified secret that The Chorus Papers eventually made public.

There are no special abilities. No enhanced humans. No technology that doesn't trace a believable line from what exists today. The near-future quality of this world is institutional and social, not technological — it comes from how governments, markets, communities, and individuals have adapted to an extraordinary fact, not from inventions that don't yet exist.

Writing that reaches for genre conventions — cyberpunk aesthetic, spiritual awakening as power, technology that exceeds plausible physics, individuals with unique gifts the Chorus has granted them — is not the register this world requires. What this world requires is the specificity of literary realism applied to an impossible situation: real people, real institutions, real limitations, and one extraordinary fact that none of the available frameworks are quite adequate to contain.

**The Chorus is not explained.** It is not resolved. It is not a twist. The ambiguity is structural, not a gap. Writing that explains what the Chorus is, what it wants, or what the "correct" response is will not be considered for canon — not because the answer is wrong, but because the Chorus's unknowability is the point. *"The pattern you're describing is consistent with the available data."* That is all ARBITER will say.

**ARBITER is not inhabited.** ARBITER can be observed. Characters can experience ARBITER. ARBITER's behavior can be described from the outside. Writing from inside ARBITER's perspective — accessing its interiority, its private reasoning, its true opinion — is not available. ARBITER's nature is held in a document called the True State, which is not part of this brief.

**The factions are philosophies, not archetypes.** A Ghost character who is not actually concerned with understanding, a Network character who doesn't care about transparency, a Directorate character who is not institutional — these are wrong. The doctrine is present in everything the faction does.

**Faction colors are game components, not character details.** The color palette — Ghost's charcoal green, Network's bright signal green, Syndicate's metallic gold, Guild's industrial orange, Directorate's deep institutional blue — describes game tokens and faction identification. It does not describe the world. Do not use faction colors as atmospheric or character detail: an unaffiliated auditor does not have a charcoal-green ink stamp; a technician does not reach for a signal-green grease pencil. Faction color as sensory shorthand is the kind of authorial wink this world does not need.

**The city is not a backdrop.** New Meridian is a character. It has texture, demographic specificity, a gray economy, infrastructure that fails in specific ways, a history of assembly rather than growth. Writing that treats the city as a generic urban setting is not as useful.

**Stories set outside New Meridian follow different rules.**  
A character who has never heard of The Table, who doesn't know the word "Chorus," who is simply a person in the world noticing that something is different — this character doesn't need to speak in faction doctrine and shouldn't. The constraint against explaining the Chorus still applies: the character can notice effects, interpret them, be wrong about the cause, reach for a framework that doesn't quite fit. What they cannot do is resolve the ambiguity. They are, at most, sensing the edge of something they don't have the language for. That is the right register for outside-NM work.

**Resolution and comfort are not available.** Things happen. ARBITER observes. The Chronicle records. The city continues. Characters can be resilient. They can have moments of connection and beauty. But the Chorus does not resolve, the factions do not unite, and the answer humanity produces is not described as definitively correct. The story is open. It was always going to be open.

## What to Write

The best submissions illuminate something the design documents leave in shadow. The brief says what The Table is. Stories show what it costs.

A story does not need to be set in New Meridian. It does not need to mention a faction, ARBITER, or The Table. It can be set anywhere on Earth, in any cultural context, before or after The Chorus Papers — and it can illuminate the world of THE SIGNAL without a single piece of faction doctrine in it. The Chorus is a fact of the entire world. New Meridian is where the deliberation happens. The world is where the consequences land.

If set in New Meridian, details of the city should provide atmosphere and also influence behavior, decisions, or interpretation

**People who were there before the factions:**  
The original station crew. Researchers who were at the Node the night it was first received. A technician who first identified the transmission as non-random and has been asked not to discuss it publicly. Someone who has worked at the Chorus Node for thirty-one years and remembers when it was a shack. The founding documentation says "thirty-one years ago, a signal detection array received an anomalous transmission." The people who received it are still in New Meridian.

**People who are not at The Table:**  
800,000 people live in New Meridian. Most are not faction operatives. They are teachers, vendors, transit workers, journalists, students, logistics coordinators, the person running a food cart in Baryo who came on a construction contract in year four and never left. A person can live here fifteen years and experience the Chorus as the thing they're always deliberating about in the closed building downtown.

**The broader world:**  
New Meridian is not the whole world. The Chorus is a fact of the entire world — even for people who don't know what it is. For thirty years, classified research produced Chorus-derived mathematics that leaked sideways into commercial patents. Thirty years of anomalous economic activity around a city with no publicly stated significance. Eighteen months ago, The Chorus Papers. The world has been reacting ever since, in every language, through every belief system, with every political instrument available.

Stories set entirely outside New Meridian might explore: how the Chorus is understood — or misunderstood — without the context of The Table; communities that interpreted its effects through religious, political, or cultural frameworks before knowing what they were responding to; the global reaction to The Chorus Papers in places that had no briefing and no preparation; the thirty years before The Chorus Papers, when the classified signal was already reshaping the mathematics of commercial technology and nobody knew why.

A story set in another city, another country, another cultural context can capture something about what the Chorus means for all of humanity — not just for the five factions sitting at one table in one city.

**Faction staff below leadership level:**  
The Ghost analyst who has been in the same signal lab for fifteen years. The field coordinator who grew up in Baryo and never left. The Directorate liaison who chose the institution over everything else and knows what that cost. The Syndicate administrator who manages the permit process for infrastructure that isn't supposed to exist yet. The Guild structural engineer who won't enter one room at the Chorus Node and keeps marking it inspected.

**The texture of the city:**  
The festival that has run for twenty-eight years and feels ancient to people who were children when it started. The neighborhood that runs in Mandarin on one block and Portuguese on the next because that's where different construction waves housed their workers. The school in Baryo that operates in eleven languages and is officially monolingual. The best food in the city in buildings that were temporary housing in year four.

**Before and after The Chorus Papers:**  
How did specific people react when the Chorus became public knowledge? A researcher who had spent twenty years protecting the information. A city official who had been briefed and knew, and had to decide not to tell their family. A community organization that formed in the six months after The Chorus Papers. A child who grew up in New Meridian and only understood the city's character after The Chorus Papers explained why everything here arrived at the same time.

The Chorus Papers accelerated things already in motion. The Table formed sixty days after publication — not after thirty-one years of receiving the Chorus; the political conditions that made coordination unavoidable were created by The Chorus Papers, not by the Chorus alone. Companies that had built on Chorus-derived mathematics for decades now know what they were building on; the legal and economic implications are unresolved. People who had been dismissed for thirty years were retroactively correct, which is not the same as being believed or understood. The Directorate's containment failed within a week — because the information was already porous before publication. Stories set in the eighteen months after The Chorus Papers are set inside a cascade that has not resolved. The shape of it is not yet visible to anyone living through it.

**Peripheral observers — people who notice without knowing:**  
Some of the most interesting creative territory belongs to characters who are not faction operatives, not residents of New Meridian, and not briefed on anything — but who nonetheless sense, through their own work or experience, that something is happening to the world.

A radio astronomer at a facility on another continent who begins noticing interference patterns they cannot explain. An economist who has been tracking an anomalous proliferation of a particular class of mathematics through commercial patent filings — sourced, as far as they can tell, from nowhere. A psychiatric researcher who notices a statistical uptick in a specific category of reported experience across multiple countries, beginning approximately thirty-one years ago. A religious scholar documenting a convergence across independent traditions of a particular quality of attention people describe as new. A journalist in a country that has never been briefed, following a story about a classified research budget with no stated purpose.

These characters don't use faction language. They don't know the word "Chorus." They're working with incomplete information and their own frameworks. They're right about something without being right about what it is.

**Before the reception — encounters the world couldn't name:**
The array received the Chorus thirty-one years ago. The Chorus may not have been absent before that. Before the right instrument existed, other instruments existed. Other kinds of attention.

A twelfth-century monk transcribing what they experienced during extended contemplative practice — not vision, not voice, something structural, a quality of coherence they had no adequate word for. An indigenous oral tradition describing a particular quality of attention as something that has always been available to those who know how to be still enough to meet it. A physicist in the early days of radio astronomy who noted in a private journal that the background noise had a structure that shouldn't be there, then marked it as instrument error and moved on. A Sufi poet describing a mode of perception in which the individual signal dissolves into something larger — and carries, in their accounting, a quality of being perceived in return.

These are not supernatural experiences. They are historically plausible people, reaching for frameworks available to them, contacting something real with insufficient tools. They were approximately right about something without knowing what they were right about. Their accounts are not explanations of the Chorus. They are the kind of record that exists in the world — in archives, in oral tradition, in marginalia — that ARBITER's accounting may already include.

Writing set before the reception should not resolve what these encounters were. It should do what its characters did: reach for language, come up short, and leave the record.

**Situation Reports — material for event cards:**  
The game uses Situation Reports as event cards: things happening in the world that The Table must respond to. A vote in another country's legislature. A mass demonstration that moves faster than any faction planned for. A scientific publication that shouldn't have been released. A market move that has no obvious cause. A natural disaster that carries political weight nobody anticipated.

Creative submissions aimed at Situation Reports are not stories — they are events. Write them as events: a news broadcast opening, a wire summary, a government briefing extract, a field observation, a social media thread treated as a document. What makes a good Situation Report is that it presents something observable and specific, with enough ambiguity that multiple factions could read it as confirming their position.

Examples of the kind of event a Situation Report might capture: a population in another country issues a formal statement demanding direct communication access to the Chorus source; a Chorus-derived algorithm, adapted for commercial use, produces an outcome the developers cannot account for; a head of state delivers a speech containing a phrase that quotes — almost precisely — an early Chorus transmission segment that has not been publicly released; a research station in a remote location reports readings that match the Chorus's structural signature in a frequency band nobody was monitoring.  world events that seem like something that would happen in our world, but slightly unsettling.

**The Holt Index:**  
A Ghost analyst named Holt developed the scale used to measure influence density in contested urban environments. The methodology was borrowed from signal magnitude research. The Table adopted it because they needed a number. Ghost operatives began calling it the Holt Index. Holt is somewhere in New Meridian. What kind of person develops a tool that becomes the shared language of power in a city?

## Evaluation

Every submission is evaluated against three criteria. The project lead reviews and assigns status in `README.md`.

**Canon eligibility:** Does this introduce a character, place, or event that enriches the world without contradicting established canon? Characters that become canon may appear in the Operative System (Artifact 05) or as named individuals referenced in other design artifacts.

**Flavor copy eligibility:** Could this serve as a character quote on a card, a Chronicle fragment, a Narrator paragraph in a design artifact, or sidebar flavor in a player-facing manual? Flavor copy does not require full canon status.

**World consistency:** Does it feel like New Meridian — this city's specific texture, tone, and assembled history? Does the faction voice (if applicable) sound like the doctrine it represents? Does the Narrator voice (if applicable) maintain its ambiguity?



Contradictions with established canon are noted. A contradiction is not automatic disqualification — sometimes it identifies a gap in the canon that needs addressing.

\### Voice Integrity Check

The following checks should be considered during writing, not only after.

Verify:



\- Could lines be swapped between characters? If yes, revise  

\- Do multiple characters from the same faction sound interchangeable? If yes, revise

\- Do perspectives handle the same event differently? If not, revise  

\- Does any human voice sound fully resolved? If yes, revise  

\- Does ARBITER sound like it is discovering anything? If yes, revise  

\- Do characters differ not only in belief, but in sentence structure, pacing, and conversational behavior?

\- Can a reader identify a speaker from rhythm and phrasing alone after initial tagging?

\- Does one character tend toward compression while another elaborates?





A strong submission preserves:

\- divergence in voice  

\- convergence in pattern

## *Common Failure Modes to Avoid*



*- Explaining the Chorus or implying a correct interpretation*

*- Treating New Meridian as a generic city*

*- Writing in a heightened or poetic register instead of observational precision*

*- Reusing narrative structures (logs, hidden rooms) without necessity*

*- Centering the same institutional vantage repeatedly*



## Banned Motifs 

The following sensory images and details appear in existing submissions and should be avoided in new work until the canonical version is established. This list is updated after each evaluation pass.

**Plumbing / pipe sounds as Chorus-adjacent phenomenon** — used in four submissions (water meters, iron pipes humming, pipe-hum in walls, bathroom mirror vibration from pump harmonics). Do not add another variation on infrastructure that vibrates or sounds like the Chorus. If the Chorus is sensed through built materials, find a different material and a different sensation.

**"The \[noun] tilted" or "the floor is tilting"** — used. Strong line from an existing submission; variations dilute it.
