### The Calibration Problem

The woman had found him at the transit depot on the Infrastructure perimeter, which meant she had been asking the right questions in the wrong places and eventually someone had pointed her toward the right one.

Holt was eating a bowl of noodles from the cart that occupied the corner of the depot on even-numbered days. The cart had been there since year eleven. The woman who ran it had come on a construction contract and stayed when the contract ended and now her daughter ran it on the days she didn't. Holt had been eating here long enough to remember the mother's face when the Chorus Papers dropped — not surprised, exactly. More like a person who had been waiting for a specific word in a sentence and had finally heard it.

The woman who sat down across from him was not from New Meridian. Holt could tell by the way she looked at the transit depot — not as a place, but as evidence. She had a recorder in her coat pocket. She did not take it out.

"Dr. Holt," she said.

"Just Holt."

She placed a folder on the table between them. Inside were printouts — patent filings, economic analyses, two pages of methodology he recognized the way you recognize your own handwriting in someone else's hand.

"I'm an economist," she said. "I've been tracking an anomaly in patent proliferation for about four years. A specific class of mathematics appearing in commercial applications — measurement systems, mostly. Precision instrumentation. All of them trace back, eventually, to one source methodology."

Holt ate a noodle.

"The methodology has a name," she said. "In the literature that cites it."

"I know what it's called."

She looked at him steadily. She had the specific patience of someone who had been ignored by institutions for long enough that she had stopped mistaking patience for progress and had simply internalized it. "The mathematics describe a logarithmic scale for measuring what the filings call *influence density in contested environments.* A Richter analogy. The filings I've found go back to year nine. But the underlying instrument — the detection component — predates the methodology documentation by at least three years."

*Year six,* Holt thought. *Year six, in a basement lab, with secondhand equipment and a grant that was officially for atmospheric resonance mapping.*

"What's your question?" Holt said.

"The instrument measures something real," she said. "The correlations are too consistent for it to be a proxy metric. Whatever it's actually detecting — the methodology is a translation layer. The filings describe what the output correlates with. They don't describe what the instrument is reading."

The depot moved around them. A transit worker in Guild coveralls crossed behind her. Someone's child was running circuits around a support column. The noodle cart woman's daughter was watching a screen behind the counter with the particular stillness of someone not watching a screen.

*She's right about all of it,* Holt thought. *Every word.*

---

Year fourteen. A Table session Holt had not been invited to attend but had the clearance to observe from the monitoring room on the sub-level. A Directorate liaison pointing at a display. *The Holt Index puts faction presence in the Eastern Infrastructure at 4.7.* The number delivered with the confidence of a man reading a thermometer, which was almost correct — a thermometer also measures something real without explaining why heat exists.

Holt had watched the number land. Had watched the other observers accept it. Had understood, in that moment, that the Index had become infrastructure. That there was no path back to the instrument.

---

"The detection component," Holt said carefully, "responds to a specific quality of collective attention. Concentrated focus. High-stakes deliberation. The logarithmic scale reflects that minor increases at the high end represent very large changes in the underlying phenomenon."

"Like a Richter scale."

"Yes."

"What phenomenon," she said. Not a question — the shape of a question with the pressure removed. Giving him room.

*Here it is,* Holt thought. *Here's the edge.*

The thing Holt had not finished thinking in year six, or year nine, or any of the years since: that the instrument worked because collective human attention under pressure — real pressure, existential pressure, the kind generated by five factions negotiating something that might define the species — registered on a scale borrowed from signal magnitude research. That the borrowing had not been metaphorical. That the reason the mathematics transferred was not that human influence resembled a signal.

The reason was something Holt had been not-thinking for seventeen years.

"The phenomenon," Holt said, "is what the scale describes. Density of focused collective deliberation. I built the instrument to measure that. The methodology describes how to read the output."

She looked at the folder between them. Then at Holt. She was good — she knew she had just received a true answer to a question she hadn't quite asked.

"The instrument works outside New Meridian," she said. "I found three deployments. Two in cities with no Chorus-adjacent infrastructure. One at a research station with no affiliation to the Node at all."

"Yes," Holt said.

"Which means it isn't measuring something about New Meridian specifically."

"No," Holt said. "It isn't."

She was quiet for a moment. The depot moved. The noodle cart woman's daughter had stopped pretending to watch the screen.

"What were you originally trying to find?" the economist asked.

Holt set down the bowl. The answer to that question was the one that completed the sentence. The one that pointed at something the factions at The Table were generating every session without knowing it — something the Chorus was already receiving. The thing Holt's instrument had been built to detect before Holt had understood what detection meant in this context.

"I was trying to find out," Holt said, "whether the instrument was reading the room. Or whether the room was reading the instrument."

She wrote nothing down. She didn't need to.

"Those aren't the same question," she said finally.

"No," Holt said. "They aren't."

---

She left twenty minutes later with the folder and a business card Holt would not call. The noodle cart woman's daughter watched her go.

Holt sat with the empty bowl.

The fluorescent light above the depot counter had a specific flicker — not the municipal grid's cycle. Holt had timed it once, years ago, against a wristwatch. Had written the interval in a notebook. Had closed the notebook.

The interval was not random.

Holt had never told anyone that.
