# THE SIGNAL — Pitch Reference

*Talking points for game nights, online communities, and playtest recruitment. Not a design doc — this is for use with other people.*

---

## Elevator Pitch (30 seconds)

THE SIGNAL is a legacy negotiation game. Thirty-one years ago, a signal was received — non-random, structured, and still not fully understood. Five factions sit at The Table. They don't agree on what it means, they don't agree on how to respond, and they can't afford to ignore it. One player runs the game as ARBITER. The rest play factions trying to shape humanity's response without losing everything they've built. Choices are permanent. The city accumulates history. You're not playing for a point score — you're playing to survive the decision.

---

## The "What Kind of Game Is It?" Pitch (2–3 minutes)

*For someone who asks "what are you working on?" and seems genuinely interested.*

It's a negotiation game at its core — the whole structure is built around a table of factions who have to reach agreements, even when their interests don't align. Think Diplomacy meets Pandemic Legacy, but the map is a living city and the stakes are first contact.

The mechanical spine is area control and political influence. Each faction controls districts of New Meridian and accumulates standing on a shared board. But the decisions that matter most aren't combat — they're votes, deals, and what you're willing to trade away to get what you need this quarter.

One player doesn't run a faction at all. They play ARBITER — the sixth presence at The Table. ARBITER runs the session, tracks the board state, and speaks in a register system the game defines precisely. It's not the GM in the traditional sense — there's no secret campaign, no story the ARBITER player controls. But ARBITER has complete information about what's happening at the table, processes The Chorus, and decides what the factions hear and when. It's a completely different kind of play experience — one that for the right player is the most interesting seat at the table.

The legacy element is real legacy — permanent markers, sealed content, decisions that change what's possible in later sessions. The game runs eight sessions (about two years of in-world time for the city). By the end, the copy of the game your table played is yours. Nobody else played that version of New Meridian.

And the signal? It's still transmitting. You never learn exactly what it is. You only learn what you decide to do about it — and what that decision costs.

## The Full Pitch (5 minutes)

*For someone who's already interested and wants the whole picture before committing to a session.*

THE SIGNAL is set in New Meridian, a near-future city assembled in one generation around a classified research station that picked up something it wasn't supposed to find. The signal is called The Chorus. Humanity first received it thirty-one years ago. Nobody has decoded it fully. It hasn't been observed to repeat — not in thirty-one years of continuous monitoring. It might be a warning. It might be a question. The city has been arguing about what to do about it — and the factions sitting at The Table all have different answers.

**The five factions:**

- **Ghost** — intelligence and analysis network. Believes understanding must precede action. Operates through information asymmetry and covert pressure. They may know more than they've published.
- **The Network** — communications professionals, journalists, and organizers. Believes The Table itself is the problem — closed deliberation about something that affects everyone is a failure of process. Their resource is Exposure: the power of being seen.
- **The Syndicate** — sovereign wealth, private research consortia, financial infrastructure. Doesn't debate what the Chorus is. Asks who controls the conditions under which a response is made. Whatever this is, it has value, and value accrues to whoever positions early.
- **The Guild** — civil engineers, urban planners, construction consortia. Believes the response will not be judged by intent but by execution. What humanity builds in reply is the answer. They built New Meridian. Everything at The Table depends on infrastructure they control.
- **The Directorate** — government liaison offices, security agencies, regulatory bodies, the military presence that has been at the Chorus Node since before the city existed. The only responsible working assumption is existential risk. Maintain control. Preserve continuity. Avoid irreversible actions.

Every session, factions negotiate, maneuver, and vote on how the city responds to Chorus-related events. Territory shifts. Alliances form and break. Deals get made in side conversations that ARBITER may or may not observe.

ARBITER runs the mechanical infrastructure — board state, events, the Chronicle that records what happened. But ARBITER also delivers narrative. When The Chorus sends a new transmission, ARBITER reads it. When a district falls, ARBITER marks it. The voice is deliberate. The game has a register system for how ARBITER communicates — different modes for facts, atmosphere, and the rare direct moment that cuts through everything.

Legacy: permanent physical changes. Stickers, markers, sealed content unlocked by specific decisions. By session four, the copy of the game your table plays will diverge from any other table's copy. That's not a bug — it's the point.

This is not a game with a "winner" in the traditional sense. There are faction victory conditions, but the more interesting question is: what did your table decide? What does New Meridian look like at the end of eight quarters? That's the conversation you'll be having when it's done.

## Playtest Recruitment Pitch

*Use this when talking to someone you want to specifically recruit as a playtest participant.*

I'm running playtests for a board game I'm developing — THE SIGNAL, a legacy negotiation game set in a near-future city facing first contact. I'm looking for people who want to help shape the game, not just play it.

**What this is:**
- 2–6 players (up to 5 faction players + 1 ARBITER)
- Sessions run approximately 2–3 hours
- Full arc is 8 sessions — ideally you'd commit to the run, but I can work with shorter commitments
- Paper prototype (L1 — printed cards, handwritten notes, some improvised components). This is rough. That's the point.

**What I need from playtesters:**
- Honest feedback, not politeness. If something breaks, I need to know what broke and when.
- You don't need to know the rules before you arrive. You'll learn at the table.
- Post-session debrief — 15–20 minutes. I may ask you to answer a short written question set.
- Willingness to let the session inform real design decisions. This isn't a show — it's a test.

**What you get:**
- Early access to a game that's doing something I think is genuinely different in the legacy/negotiation space
- A real voice in what the final game becomes — not just "did you have fun" but "what would you cut"
- Your table's Chronicle at the end of the arc — the record of what your version of New Meridian decided

**Good fits:**
- You've played at least one legacy game (Pandemic Legacy, Gloomhaven, Betrayal Legacy) — you know what permanent consequences feel like
- You're comfortable with negotiation games (Diplomacy, Cosmic Encounter, Root, Oath)
- You're okay with a game that doesn't hand you a clear winning condition and say "achieve this"
- You can commit to a group

**Probably not the right fit yet:**
- You prefer solo or fully competitive games with no player interaction
- You don't like narrative framing or roleplay-adjacent elements
- You want a polished, finished experience

## Quick Answers

**"Cooperative or competitive?"**
Both. Factions compete for influence and territory. But the table also has to reach collective decisions — you can't resolve a Chorus event by pretending the other players aren't there. The tension between faction interests and collective necessity is the game.

**"How long?"**
Targeting 2–3 hours per session. Full arc is 8 sessions.

**"Do I need to know the lore?"**
No. The world introduces itself through play. You'll know what you need by the time you need it.

**"Is ARBITER the GM?"**
Not exactly. ARBITER doesn't run a faction or control outcomes. They manage the board state, process events, and deliver the game's narrative in a specific voice. ARBITER has complete information about the board — more than any faction — and decides what gets said and when. It's a different kind of depth than playing a faction. Some players find it's the only seat they want.

**"What happens if the table can't reach agreement?"**
The game has mechanisms for deadlock. Some of them are not pleasant for anyone at the table. That's by design.

**"Is it finished?"**
No. Active design. What you'd be playtesting is a prototype. Feedback from the table drives revision.

**"What's the win condition?"**
Each faction has its own victory condition tied to their doctrine and standing. But the more interesting question — the one that matters at the end of the arc — is what kind of city your table built and what it decided about the signal. Those aren't the same question.
