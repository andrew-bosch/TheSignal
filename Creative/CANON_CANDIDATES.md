### ⭐ "The Third Desk" — vignette

**Source:** Gemini20260516_1.md
**Status:** 📝 Canon candidate — Gabriel (Ministry of Commerce auditor) is a viable minor character. Setting (Infrastructure perimeter, Commerce ministry) enriches the world. Subject (Chorus mathematics bleeding into commercial patents, administrative layer of New Meridian) is exactly right.

**Required edits before consideration:**
1. Line: *"leaving a faint, charcoal-green imprint on the top application"* — remove the color. "Leaving a faint imprint" is sufficient.
2. Lines about piezo-microphone and the throat-clearing sound — trim. The effect is better implied than instrumented.

**Text (with edits marked):**

---

The fluorescent tube above Gabriel's desk had a three-beat flicker that didn't match the municipal grid's cycle. He knew this because he had timed it against his wristwatch—a mechanical piece his grandfather had carried out of Munich before the construction waves began in year seven.

Gabriel was an auditor for the Ministry of Commerce, Grade 3. His office sat on the outer perimeter of the Infrastructure, where the concrete walls still smelled faintly of the damp lime used during the hurried pours of year twelve. On his desk lay forty-two applications for sub-harmonic cooling arrays, all filed by shell companies registered in maritime jurisdictions that didn't exist thirty-one years ago.

"Look at the curve on the dissipation constant," Gabriel said, not looking up.

Across the aisle, Elena didn't stop typing. She was nineteen when the leak happened; she wore the bright signal green pins of the Network on her lanyard, though she tucked them inside her collar when the Directorate inspectors walked the floor. "They're just heat pumps, Gabe. People want their milk to stay cold in the Sprawl."

"A heat pump moves energy from one box to another," Gabriel murmured, tracing a column of notation with a blunt fingernail. The mathematics in the filings were elegant, recursive, and wholly unnecessary for refrigeration. They looked like a poem written in the language of fluid dynamics, designed to drop the ambient temperature of a room by precisely four percent. ~~while leaving a residual vibration in the copper piping that sounded, if one attached a piezo-microphone, like a throat clearing.~~ "These don't move it. They invite it to leave."

He knew where the formulas came from. Everyone in Commerce knew now, even if the official manual still referred to them as "Anomalous Secondary Efficiencies." For thirty years, these numbers had dripped through the research complex downtown into the patent office like grease from an over-oiled engine. Now that the secret was eighteen months detached from its cage, the drops had become a river.

A man from the Syndicate had visited Gabriel's supervisor Tuesday morning. He hadn't worn gold—that was for the boardrooms in the Core—but his coat was made of a wool that didn't catch the gray dust of New Meridian's gravel roads. He had left a folder on the desk. Not a bribe. Just an advanced logistics schedule for a foundry being built in the Portuguese-dominant sector of the Sprawl.

Gabriel lifted his stamp. The ink pad was dry, leaving a faint imprint on the top application: *PENDING FURTHER CLARIFICATION.* ~~[charcoal-green removed]~~

"They're building the foundations before we approve the permits," Gabriel said.

Elena finally stopped typing. The silence between her keystrokes felt heavy, full of the weight of the city outside their window—800,000 people breathing air that had been analyzed down to the molecule and still tasted of wet cement.

"They aren't waiting for the permit, Gabe," she said quietly. "They're just waiting for the concrete to dry."

### ⭐ "The Dry Run" — vignette

**Source:** Gemini20260516_2.md
**Status:** 📝 Canon candidate — Aris Thorne (Atacama radio astronomer) is a viable peripheral observer character. The outside-NM setting is exactly the brief's "most interesting creative territory." The absence-not-signal framing is correct and careful.

**Required edits before consideration:**
1. "four thousand miles from the political panic of New Meridian" → remove "political panic." Flat voice: "four thousand miles from New Meridian."
2. "signal-green grease pencil" → remove the color. "her grease pencil balanced between her fingers" is sufficient.

**Text (with edits marked):**

---

The ink on the strip-chart recorder hadn't changed formula since 1974, but the paper was curling differently.

Dr. Aris Thorne didn't use his reading glasses; he used a jeweler's loupe to inspect the blue ink line tracking the hydrogen line background from Sector 4. The observatory sat in the high, cold desert of the Atacama, four thousand miles from ~~the political panic of~~ New Meridian, and six thousand miles from anything resembling an executive order.

"It's not an atmospheric bounce, Aris," Maya said from the lower equipment bay. She was swapping out the liquid helium dewars for the front-end receivers. Her knuckles were scarred from slip-wrenches and frozen brass. "I ran the diagnostic twice. The cryogenic seals are tight. The thermal noise is where it should be."

"The thermal noise is flat," Aris said, his voice level, carrying the dry scraping quality of the gravel outside. "But the baseline has teeth."

He dropped the loupe into his palm. On the chart, the pen had tracked a steady, recursive dip—exactly three-point-eight percent below the expected galactic background—occurring every four hundred and twelve seconds. It wasn't a transmission. It was an absence. A minor, clean hesitation in the universe's natural static, as if something incredibly heavy had briefly stepped between the dish and the sky, then moved on without leaving a footprint.

"The Ministry of Posts sent a courier from Santiago this morning," Maya said, her boots clanging up the steel ladder. "Not a wire. A man in a wool coat with a leather pouch. He wanted the raw magnetic tapes from Tuesday."

Aris didn't look toward the door where the courier's jeep was still idling in the heat haze. He looked at his own fingers, stained with the violet dye of the chart ink. For thirty years, the funding for this array had arrived through five different shell corporations registered in maritime jurisdictions that didn't exist when he was a graduate student. He had always assumed it was drug money or tax evasion.

"Did you give them the tapes?" Aris asked.

"I gave him the calibration runs from last November," Maya said. She sat on the edge of the desk, her ~~signal-green~~ grease pencil balanced between her fingers. "He didn't know the difference. He looked like he wanted to be somewhere else. Somewhere with water."

Aris turned the dial on the receiver panel. The speaker above the rack didn't hiss; it hummed with a low, recursive rhythm that sounded like water moving through old iron pipes.

"They aren't looking for a signal anymore, Maya," Aris murmured, watching the ink pen begin its predictable, four-hundred-second descent. "They're just checking to see if we've noticed the floor is tilting."

### ⭐ "The Shack" — vignette + character

**Source:** Gemini20260516_3.md
**Status:** 📝 Canon candidate — strongest character produced so far. **Text (with edits marked):**

---

The original detection array didn't have a floor. It had a gravel bed poured directly into a timber frame to keep the moisture from the valley floor from rising into the receiver racks.

Dr. Alistair Vance sat on a three-legged milking stool he had carried up the ridge himself during the first week of the installation. He was seventy-one now, but his fingers still moved with the precise, rhythmic hesitation of a man who spent thirty-one years tuning vacuum tube assemblies that the Core bureaucracy had long since classified as obsolete junk. His current office was on the sub-level of the Chorus Node complex, two hundred meters below the spot where the shack had stood. The walls here were polished aggregate, cool and clean, but the air still tasted faintly of parched transformer oil if you sat close enough to the intake vents.

"The logbooks for October of year one aren't in the central repository, Alistair," Dr. Lin said. She didn't use the terminal; she stood by the heavy security door, her lapel pin ~~charcoal-green~~ pinned flat against her wool vest. Ghost didn't usually ask questions this late in the quarter, but the response window was two years out, and the Table was getting loud. "The Directorate says they were scrubbed during the reorganization in year nine."

"They weren't scrubbed," Vance said, his voice dry as old parchment. He didn't look up from the soldering iron he was cleaning. "They were written on yellow legal pads. The glue dried out by year twelve. If you want to know what the third channel looked like before the Chorus settled into the current recursive cadence, you don't look at the data banks. You look at the copper grounding rods outside."

"The ones under the plaza?"

"The ones thirty feet below the plaza," Vance corrected. He set the iron down on its ceramic rest. The click of the metal against porcelain was the only sound in the room for four seconds. "We didn't have the shielding then. When the transmission started, it didn't just register on the strip charts. It ran down the chassis. It cooked the worms right out of the dirt around the shack. For three days, the whole ridge smelled like turned earth and burnt copper."

Lin shifted her weight. "The Directorate brief says the initial reception was silent."

"The Directorate wasn't here until month two," Vance said softly. He reached out to turn a dial on an ancient, uncoupled frequency analyzer. The needle didn't move, but the casing gave off a low, familiar hum—a vibration that had lived in his teeth since the night the sky stopped being quiet. "The array didn't make a sound. But the mountain did. If you put your ear to the timber frame, you could hear the bolts groaning against the grain. Like someone was tightening a vise from the other side of the ridge."

He looked at Lin then. ~~[cut: "his eyes gray and unclouded by the institutional panic that had consumed the Core since the leak"]~~

"They're upstairs trying to vote on an answer," Vance said, turning back to his bench. "They haven't even figured out that the shack was built on the wrong hill. The signal wasn't aiming for the array, Lin. The array just happened to be standing in the way when the room tilted."

**Required edits before consideration:**

**Text (with edit marked):**

---

The water in Director Kang's glass did not ripple when Representative Miller struck the table.

It should have. The conference table was three meters of solid, unbraced structural oak, delivered during the year-nine expansion when the Directorate still controlled the timber requisitions for the Core. When Miller brought his palm down against the grain, the impact made a sharp, dry crack that died before it reached the plaster molding of the ceiling.

"Sixteen months of redacting the infrastructure logs won't change what the Sprawl already knows, Kang," Miller said. His voice carried the raspy edge of a man who spent his nights behind a pirate transmitter in the Portuguese sector, breathing the gray dust of the unpaved perimeter roads. "The Network has the telemetry from the year-four water mains. The pressure isn't dropping because of faulty gaskets. The grid is hesitating."

Kang did not look up from her folder. The documents inside were printed on heavy, high-rag stock that didn't rattle when the ventilation shifted. "The municipal water supply remains within nominal regulatory variances, Representative. Your broadsheets are mistaking an engineering cycle for an existential crisis."

Between them sat ARBITER.

It did not occupy a chair; it occupied the space where a chair had been removed during the preliminary session of the first quarter. To a casual observer, it was a matte-black housing no larger than a terminal relay, bolted directly into the oak floorboards. It had remained completely immobile for forty-seven minutes.

Then, the ambient hum of the room altered. It was not a sound so much as an absence of static—the sudden, clean drop of background noise that always accompanied a shift in the protocol registers.

[THE RECORD] "The municipal grid of New Meridian experienced four localized drops in hydraulic pressure between 02:14 and 02:18 standard time. Cumulative volume variance: twelve hundred liters. The Directorate's internal memorandum regarding these drops was finalized at 04:00. It is currently designated as an unindexed asset."

Miller let out a short, wet breath that might have been a laugh. "Tell her what else is in the memo, then."

ARBITER did not turn. It did not have an axis to rotate upon.

[THE OBSERVATION] "The air in the room is five percent drier than during the previous session. The oil on the table's iron bracketry has collected a layer of pulverized limestone from the ongoing excavation in the sub-levels. The human participants are breathing at an accelerated cadence. The correlation between atmospheric pressure and conversational velocity remains within historical parameters."

Kang finally closed her folder. The snap of the leather binder was the only sound that escaped the localized dampening. "Is that all the system has to offer for the record?"

[THE RECKONING] "You are arguing about the pipes because you lack the instruments to measure the weight of the floor. The city is not sinking, Director Kang. The mountain is simply becoming more specific."

The silence that followed was not the quiet of an empty room. ~~[cut: elaborated engine/altitude simile — stop here]~~
