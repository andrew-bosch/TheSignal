### The Restaurant That Stayed

The owner still calls it temporary housing in his head.

He doesn’t say that out loud anymore.

The four of them move through the same small choreography: the owner at the counter, the cook at the flat-top, the host near the door where the plastic partition used to be, and Mr. Alvarez in his usual seat—third table, right side, back to the wall.

It’s late enough that the rush has broken but early enough that no one is really leaving.

The owner takes the plate out himself.

"How is everything tonight?" he asks, setting it down like he always does—left edge first, then turning it slightly so the protein faces center.

Mr. Alvarez lifts his eyes instead of the fork.

"It’s good," he says. Then, after a second, "Consistent."

The cook makes a quiet sound that could be approval or correction.

"You’re getting the garlic wrong," she says from the flat-top, not looking up. "Not burnt, not raw. There’s a middle."

"I like it," Mr. Alvarez says. "However it is."

The host steps in then, smiling in the automatic way that still reads as real.

"Can I get you anything else? More water?"

There’s already a full glass on the table.

Mr. Alvarez looks at it, then back at her.

"No," he says, and then, after a pause that doesn’t quite belong there, "I think that’s enough."

The owner stays instead of stepping away.

He watches Mr. Alvarez take a bite.

"We changed suppliers again," he says. "Same cut, different source. Prices are—" He stops. "Different."

"Everything is," the host says, too quickly, and then laughs a small correction into it. "I mean—since last year."

The cook turns something over on the grill. Oil lifts, catches the light, settles.

"It was always like this," she says. "We just didn’t keep talking about it."

Mr. Alvarez chews. Takes his time with it, like he’s deciding something that isn’t about the food.

"People used to explain things," he says finally. "Even if they were wrong." He sets his fork down. "Now they stop halfway through."

The host shifts her weight.

"Well," she says, "maybe we just…"

She doesn’t finish it.

The owner nods anyway, like she did.

"Business is stable," he offers. "Not up, not down. Same people, mostly. New ones sometimes. They sit down like they already know the place." He looks at the table, the plate, the glass of water. "Like it’s been here longer than it has."

"It has for them," the cook says. "Everyone’s timeline is different now."

"That’s not—" the owner starts, then stops.

Mr. Alvarez picks up his fork again.

"My daughter asked me last week," he says, "why the city was built all at once." He takes another bite, smaller this time. "She’s ten. They told her in school, I think. Or she heard it somewhere and decided it was a question she could ask me."

"What did you say?" the host asks.

"I said contracts," he says. "Jobs. Opportunity. That’s what it felt like at the time." He looks up at the owner. "That’s what you told us, right?"

The owner nods. It’s the correct motion.

"It wasn’t wrong," he says.

The cook wipes the edge of the flat-top with a cloth that’s already clean.

"It wasn’t complete," she says.

No one disagrees.

The host glances toward the door, where someone new is standing just inside, looking around like they’re trying to match what they see with something they expected.

"Give me a minute," she says, and slips away.

The owner stays.

"We’ve been here twenty-seven years," he says. "Same space. Same lease, more or less. We changed the sign twice. The wiring three times." He pauses. "The conversation—" He stops there too. "It used to be easier."

Mr. Alvarez nods, but not in agreement.

"It used to end," he says.

The cook finally looks up.

"Nothing ends," she says. "You just stop following it."

From the door, the host’s voice carries back, warm and practiced.

"Table for one?"

A pause. Then:

"Yes," the new customer says. "I think so."

The owner watches them cross the room.

"They always say that first," he says quietly. "‘I think.’"

No one responds to that.

Mr. Alvarez takes another bite, then sets the fork down again as if the action has lost its place in the sequence.

"It’s good," he says, almost to himself this time. "Consistent."

The cook turns back to the grill.

"Finish it before it gets cold," she says.

He does.

No one asks anything else that requires an answer.
