They schedule the buses as if the city were stable.

On paper, it is. The routes repeat. The headways hold. The software produces a kind of confidence—intervals that look like decisions. Mara reviews them in the depot office that used to be a warehouse with temporary shelving, the bolts still visible where bunks once stood. The system flags variance. It never names causes.

She arrived in year nine, when the second construction wave needed drivers who could start in winter. She learned the city by what it demanded at 6:10 a.m.: school runs in three languages; a hospital transfer that ignored the posted route; a line that appeared outside a building that had not been there the week before. The Core didn’t concern her then. The Core still doesn’t, exactly. The Core is a place buses approach carefully and leave on time.

The problem is the 43.

It doesn’t fail. It drifts. Ridership increases on segments that do not connect to anything obvious. Alightings cluster near a substation that has been under maintenance for six months. The drift is small enough to be absorbed and large enough to recur. The report calls it “localized instability with non-random distribution.” The fix, if there is one, is not in the system.

Mara walks the line on her break, in the hour that belongs to no one. The city at that time is not asleep; it is between explanations. A shopfront cycles through three languages on a display that assumes a different audience every ten seconds. A courier waits at a corner, checking a device that does not request confirmation when it routes him across the street, back again, and then further south.

At stop 17, the shelter glass has been replaced with a material the maintenance log describes as “temporary,” though no end date is recorded. The glass holds the street in a way that does not distort it, exactly, but organizes it—edges align that should not. Reflections arrive before the objects themselves. Mara stands there long enough for two scheduled buses to pass without her.

The drift maps to this segment with an accuracy that suggests intention, if intention were something the city had.

She notes the time, the temperature, the pattern of arrivals. She does not note the sensation that the route is being learned—from the other side, as if the system she trusts is a copy of something more primary. She files the report with the language she is given. The language accepts it. The schedule updates by one minute.

The next morning, the 43 runs exactly on time through the segment. Ridership stabilizes. The distribution returns to baseline. The system closes the alert automatically.

Mara leaves the flag in her personal log.

She has learned that stability arrives in this city the way it does everywhere else—provisionally, and with incomplete explanations.
