They assigned him the perimeter audit because it was procedural, not interpretive.

The checklist specified distances, materials, tolerances that had been revised nineteen times in twelve years without a recorded incident that required revision. The revisions had sources — cross-departmental, multi-signature, validated — and none of them described what changed between versions. Only that it had.

He measured the access corridor twice.

Not because the numbers drifted. They didn’t. The corridor kept its dimensions with a consistency that made the second measurement redundant before he began it. He ran it again because the first entry in the log felt premature.

The record accepted both entries without comment.

At the inner boundary, the floor treatment changed from industrial composite to something the Guild documentation classified as “persistent substrate.” No installation date. No supplier record. No maintenance log.

His badge authorized up to the boundary and no further.

There was no visible barrier — not a door, not a field, not a fault line in the material. The boundary existed in the system that processed access requests. He placed his hand forward, not touching anything, and waited for the denial.

It arrived before contact.

He recorded the timestamp.

Behind him, the corridor continued in the same geometry toward administrative offices, observation decks, controlled labs where Ghost staff worked in rotations long enough that their names recurred in weekly summaries. In front of him, the same corridor extended without variation past the point he was not permitted to enter.

He marked the forward section as inspected.

The log required a confirmation field: _visual verification of all surfaces._

He selected yes.

There was a pause between input and record confirmation that the system documentation did not describe. It was not a delay; it was an interval. It had the quality of something being allowed to resolve.

The entry finalized.

He stood at the boundary longer than the checklist required. Not long enough to register as deviation.

On his way out, he passed the secondary monitor station where a Directorate liaison reviewed movement logs for variances that crossed threshold definitions the audit teams did not have access to. The liaison did not look up. The audit had not produced anything that required escalation.

He submitted his report at 16:32.

The system appended a note he had not entered: _Perimeter conditions consistent with prior readings._

He read it twice.

It was accurate.

It was also new.
