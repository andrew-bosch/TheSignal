## 1. Overview

### Problem This Document Solves
Physical game components communicate before a player reads a word. The color of a presence token tells a player which faction placed it. The visual weight of ARBITER's marker tells the table something is different here. The typography on a card tells a player whether they are looking at something covert or something public.

Without a design system, physical prototype construction produces visual inconsistency — and visual inconsistency produces cognitive load at the table. A player who has to look twice to confirm whose token is whose is not thinking about the Chorus.

This artifact defines the visual standards that govern every physical component and every printable visual artifact in the L1 paper prototype.

### Deliverable
- Faction color palette and identity system
- ARBITER visual identity (distinct from all five factions)
- Neutral and board color standards
- Typography standards for printable artifacts
- Component visual standards (size, shape, marking conventions)
- Card layout standards (by card type)
- Production notes for paper prototype assembly

### Success Criteria
- Any physical component can be identified by faction at a glance at normal table distance
- ARBITER's components are visually distinct from all faction components — they read as *more*, not merely *different*
- Visual hierarchy on cards reflects information hierarchy — the most important field is visually dominant
- A person assembling the prototype from this document and Artifact 09 produces a playable physical set without additional guidance

## 3. Game Purpose

Visual design in a paper prototype serves one purpose: reduce friction between the player's intentions and the game's response. Every visual element should make the board state easier to read, not harder. Every card layout should surface the right information at the right moment.

This is not an aesthetic exercise, though aesthetics matter. It is a functional specification that happens to be beautiful.

---

## 4. Narrative Function

The world of THE SIGNAL is future-punk: not utopian, not dystopian, but pressured — a city that has lived under an unresolved transmission for thirty-one years and has organized its entire political and economic life around responding to something it cannot fully understand.

The visual language of the game should reflect this: grounded, functional, slightly worn. Not sterile. Not polished. New Meridian is a real place, and the factions that operate in it are real organizations — their visual identity should feel like something that exists, not something designed to look good on a shelf.

### The Mat as Live Data Interface

The game mat is not a game board. It is a **shared data interface** — the instrument through which The Table reads the state of New Meridian and its own deliberation simultaneously. Every zone on the mat is a feed: a data stream all parties have agreed to surface collectively.

The mat's aesthetic direction follows from this: **it should look like a live analytics dashboard or public intelligence display**. Think a civic sentiment feed with faction presence as data series. Think a city-scale BI dashboard updating in real time — engagement metrics, key indicators, territorial overlays. Clean, data-forward, minimal. Not decorative.

In the paper prototype, this aesthetic is achieved through visual design choices: typography, track design, data-visualization-style indicators rather than decorative borders, faction colors functioning as data series rather than heraldry.

In the digital version (L2+), the mat becomes literally the screen The Table is looking at. The faction presence tokens animate as they are placed. The Public Standing track updates as a live feed. The Chorus Activity track pulses. The fiction and the artifact are the same object — the paper prototype was always a physical instantiation of something that wants to be electronic.

This design direction is locked. See PM02 FD-02. It governs all mat visual design decisions from L1 forward.

ARBITER is the exception to all faction visual conventions. ARBITER's visual language has a quality none of the faction materials share: it reads as *from elsewhere*. Slightly different proportions. Slightly more weight. As if it was designed by something that has processed human visual conventions and produced something almost, but not quite, like them.

## 5. Design Principles

1. **Faction identity is instant.** Any component on the board is identifiable by faction without reading a label, at arm's length.

2. **ARBITER reads as more.** ARBITER's components are not a sixth faction color. They are a visual statement. The ARBITER Dominance Marker at the Chorus Node should make players feel the weight of what it represents.

3. **Information hierarchy governs card layout.** The field that players need most often is largest and first. Flavor text is secondary. Portrait field is not visible on the player-facing card face.

4. **Paper prototype pragmatism.** L1 is a paper prototype. Materials are sourced, printed, and assembled by hand. Visual standards must be achievable with standard print-at-home, index cards, and commonly available craft supplies. Where ideal and achievable conflict, achievable wins and ideal is noted for future production.

5. **Narrative language on all components.** Every label uses in-world terms per PM03 §1. "Presence token" appears nowhere on a component — "presence" does if any label is needed. Most components should be visually self-evident without labels.

## 7. ARBITER Visual Identity

ARBITER's visual identity is not a faction color. It is a statement.

### 7.1 ARBITER Color
**White** — provisional. See D-P-02 for ARBITER Dominance Marker visual design decision.

White is chosen because:
- It reads as neither aligned nor opposed to any faction color
- It reads as absence-of-faction, which is consistent with ARBITER's nature
- In physical prototype, white poker chips and markers are universally available

### 7.2 ARBITER Dominance Marker (PM01 §2.08a)
The ARBITER Dominance Marker is a single fused piece placed at the Chorus Node at setup. It consists of:
- 8 ARBITER-keyed presence tokens (white, fused into a stack) 
- ARBITER dominance marker on top — same visual language as faction control flags but **reads as *more***: larger, slightly different proportions, differently weighted

**Design intent:** When a player looks at the Chorus Node, they should feel something before they understand it intellectually. The marker is not threatening. It is *established*. The Chorus Node has had something in it for longer than any faction has existed.

**Production note for L1:** Stack 8 white poker chips, affix with adhesive or paint. Top with a distinguishing element — oversized token, different material (metal washer, coin), or hand-marked marker that reads as categorically different from faction markers.

*[TBD — D-P-02 visual design decision required before this section is final. Consider: does the fused marker need a visual reference to the Chorus (sine wave, transmission pattern) or does its scale alone carry the meaning?]*

## 11. Card Layout Standards

*[TBD — full card layout in V07. Standards below govern field placement and hierarchy.]*

### 11.1 Card Face Hierarchy
All card types share this general hierarchy (top to bottom):
1. Card name (largest, most prominent)
2. Card type / subtype indicators (small tags, top corners)
3. Faction indicator (color border or faction mark)
4. Cost and Beat (left column, upper body)
5. Effect text (right column or full width, body)
6. Taxonomy (small, lower body — for design reference; may be omitted from player-facing card)
7. Flavor text (italic, bottom, below separator)

### 11.2 Information Hidden from Players
The Portrait field is **not present on the player-facing card face**. It appears only on ARBITER's reference copy (if any) or is held in ARBITER's private documentation (Artifact 07). This is a physical production requirement — the Portrait value cannot be visible to the player who holds the card. See 00a R30a.

## 14. Special Conditions & Gameplay Impacts

### Colorblind Accessibility
All components use secondary shape/marking differentiation in addition to color. No game-critical information is conveyed by color alone. *[Confirm with final palette before production.]*

### ARBITER Marker at Node
The ARBITER Dominance Marker is placed once and never removed. It occupies physical space at the Chorus Node that is distinct from faction presence. Its permanent presence should be a visual constant — players should stop seeing it as a separate element and start feeling it as a feature of the Node.
