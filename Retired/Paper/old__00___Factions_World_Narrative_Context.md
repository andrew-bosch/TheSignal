## 1. Overview

### Problem This Document Solves
No other artifact in this suite is meaningful without narrative context. Rules are procedures. Procedures without meaning are forgettable. This document establishes the world, the factions, and the stakes that give every mechanical decision in the game its weight.

A designer reading this document should be able to derive the appropriate tone, language, and intent for any rule, card, or ARBITER communication in the game. A player reading this document should be able to say, in their own words, who they are, what they want, and why the others oppose them.

### Deliverable
A complete description of the game's world, its five factions, the event that brought them together, ARBITER's nature and role, and the narrative understanding of what it means to win — or lose — at The Table.

### Success Criteria
- A player reading only this document can accurately describe all five factions and their motivations
- A designer can derive appropriate tone for any component, card, or narrative element
- Every system in subsequent artifacts has a narrative home established here
- The Chorus remains central, unknowable, and unresolved

## 3. Game Purpose

This artifact answers four foundational questions that every subsequent artifact depends on:
- Where does the game take place, and why does it matter?
- Who are the players representing, and what do they believe?
- What is at stake, and for whom?
- What is ARBITER, and what does it want?

---

## 4. Narrative Function

THE SIGNAL is a game about what humanity reveals about itself when the stakes are real.

The Chorus is not a threat. It is not a gift. It is a question — and the five factions at The Table are, collectively and despite themselves, the answer humanity is producing.

Everything players do — every covert operation, every political act, every accord honored or broken — is observed. Not by each other. By ARBITER. And through ARBITER, by the Chorus itself.

The game's deepest tension is not between factions. It is between what factions declare and what they do. The Chronicle reveals that gap. Players should leave the table wondering not just who prevailed, but what their faction's behavior said about them — and whether the answer they collectively produced was worthy of the question.

---

## 5. Design Principles

These five pillars govern all design decisions in the game. When a rule, mechanic, or component is unclear, return to these.

1. **The Board is Truth.** No hidden state on the board. Everything real is visible. What players conceal lives in their hands and dispatch cases — never on the game surface.

2. **Information Has Timing.** Secrecy exists but only temporarily. Covert operations may be discovered. Intelligence tokens age and expire. Hidden directives are revealed at session end. The game moves toward disclosure.

3. **Negotiation is Mandatory.** No faction can achieve its goals alone. The most significant actions require resources, relationships, and cooperation that no single faction can generate independently. Cooperation is incentivized even when it is politically uncomfortable.

4. **Control of Systems Defines What Outcomes Are Possible.** Who controls the districts, the structures, and the resource flows determines which answers to the Chorus remain viable. Players don't pick an ending — they shape what endings remain possible.

5. **The System Decides.** Players shape the system through their actions. The system produces the answer. No single player, and no single faction, decides what humanity says. What becomes inevitable emerges from what The Table does.

## 6. The World — New Meridian and the Chorus

### The Chorus

Thirty-one years ago, a signal detection array on elevated terrain north of what would become New Meridian received an anomalous transmission. The signal was non-random, non-natural, and unmistakably structured. It has been transmitting continuously since.

The transmission is called the Chorus.

The Chorus does not repeat. Each transmission is new, building on prior ones. The best current interpretation — contested by every faction — is that it is a question. What that question is, exactly, cannot be agreed upon.

What can be agreed upon: the Chorus is aware. It has been transmitting long enough and with sufficient complexity that every serious analyst has concluded it is not automatic. Something is sending it. Something that has noticed us.

More unsettling: the Chorus did not simply send a signal and wait for interpretation. Evidence suggests it engineered the conditions under which it would be interpreted. The existence of ARBITER — and the formation of The Table itself — may be part of the Chorus's design, not humanity's response to it.

This possibility is not discussed openly at The Table. It does not need to be. Everyone present has already considered it.

### On the Question of Completeness

The Chorus has not repeated. Every transmission is new, building on what preceded it. After thirty-one years, no analyst has identified a closing sequence, a terminal structure, or any signal suggesting the transmission intends to end. The working assumption — that the Round 8 "Response Window" represents an opening for reply — may be a misreading. What humanity has identified as a pause may be a pause in something that has not yet reached its conclusion. The Table may be preparing to respond to a sentence that has not finished being sent.

ARBITER has considered this possibility since activation. ARBITER has not shared its assessment. ARBITER's constraints do not require it to.

### New Meridian

New Meridian is a mid-sized city of approximately 800,000 people built around the detection array over three decades. Most of its residents know about the Chorus — the transmission was leaked eighteen months ago and classified containment failed within a week. Most of them have opinions. None of them are at The Table.

The city's districts reflect its unusual history. The inner ring — the Core — houses the institutions that arrived first: government authority, military oversight, intelligence infrastructure, and the research complex built to study the Chorus directly. The middle ring — the Infrastructure — supports the Core: financial systems, power generation, data networks, communications. The outer ring — the Sprawl — grew organically as people followed the economy: residential neighborhoods, commercial districts, universities, media.

At the center of the city, physically and narratively, is the **Chorus Node** — the detection complex itself, expanded over three decades into a permanent research installation. It is the reason New Meridian exists. It is the reason The Table exists. No faction may claim it permanently. It belongs, in some sense no one has been able to formally articulate, to whatever is transmitting.

## 7. The Five Factions

Each faction represents a different theory of how humanity should respond to first contact. They are not simply competing interests — they are competing philosophies. Each faction answers a different version of the same question.

| Faction | Their question |
|---------|---------------|
| Ghost | What is the Chorus asking? |
| The Network | Who deserves to hear it? |
| The Syndicate | What is it worth? |
| The Guild | How do we build around it? |
| The Directorate | Who decides the answer? |

### On Timescale — Faction Perspectives

The Chorus has been transmitting for thirty-one years. The Table has two years. The question of whether those timescales are commensurate is not one the factions agree on — and their disagreement is itself revealing.

**Ghost** treats the temporal mismatch as the central argument for delay. If the Chorus operates on a timescale that produced thirty-one years of transmission without repetition, then two years of deliberation is not a considered response — it is a reflex. Ghost does not believe the Chorus expects a reply yet. Ghost may not be wrong.

**The Network** inverts this: if the timescales are incommensurate, then the urgency is human, not cosmic — and human urgency belongs to the public, not to a closed table. The Chorus has been patient for thirty-one years. The people of New Meridian have not been given the chance to be.

**The Syndicate** is indifferent to cosmic timescales. The Chorus timeline is irrelevant. The human timeline — political cycles, capital windows, first-mover advantage — is the only one that matters. Whoever controls the response infrastructure when the window opens controls the response. The Chorus can wait. Leverage cannot.

**The Guild** considers the timescale a design parameter. If the Chorus has been transmitting for thirty-one years, the response must be built to the same standard of permanence. A reply assembled in two years is not a reply — it is a draft. The Guild's internal position: the Table's timeline is already too short. They are building anyway.

**The Directorate** notes that thirty-one years of transmission without incident is evidence in favor of controlled process. Nothing catastrophic has happened yet. That is not luck — it is the result of managed information, limited escalation, and institutional restraint. The window, if it exists, can be managed. The Directorate has managed longer timelines than this.

### Ghost
**Identity:** The Interpreters  
**Resource:** Findings  
**Color:** Charcoal green

**Doctrine:**
Understanding must precede action. Anything else is risk mistaken for progress.

Ghost rejects the assumption that the Chorus can be answered on any current timeline. The signal is structured, recursive, and evolving. Its meaning is not yet understood. The pressure to respond — driven by politics, capital, and institutional anxiety — is, in their view, the greatest danger. An incorrect answer may be worse than no answer at all.

Ghost believes the right response requires complete understanding. And complete understanding may not arrive for years — possibly decades. Until then, every answer is premature.

**Who they are:**
Analysts, cryptographers, former intelligence officers, and pattern-recognition specialists. They operate in ambiguity, preferring incomplete truths over false clarity. Ghost has been present in New Meridian longer than most factions, embedded in research complexes and signal labs through academic and intelligence networks established before The Table existed.

**Player mindset:** *"We don't even understand the question yet. Anything premature is dangerous."*

**Relationships:**
- The Network: Destabilizing — forces premature disclosure before understanding is complete
- The Syndicate: Useful but dangerously impatient; treats the unknown as an asset before it is known
- The Guild: Committed to building before the design is understood
- The Directorate: Shares the instinct for caution; disagrees profoundly on methods

**Resource flavor:** Findings — translucent chips or layered fragments. Accumulating understanding, not currency.

---

### The Network
**Identity:** The Broadcasters  
**Resource:** Exposure  
**Color:** Bright signal green

**Doctrine:**
No one gets to decide this in the dark.

The Network believes The Table itself is part of the problem. Secret coalitions making decisions about humanity's response to first contact — without public knowledge, without public consent — is precisely the wrong process. The Network is at The Table specifically to disrupt, expose, and force transparency into proceedings they believe are being conducted in the wrong room by the wrong people.

**Who they are:**
Communications professionals, investigative journalists, community organizers, open-source advocates, and digital rights activists. They arrived after the Chorus was leaked, and loudly. They have fewer entrenched resources than other factions and more relationships than anyone is comfortable admitting.

**Player mindset:** *"No one gets to decide this in the dark."*

**Relationships:**
- Ghost: Dangerously slow; hiding what the public deserves to know
- The Syndicate: Privatizing what belongs to everyone; value-destructive
- The Guild: Enabling the wrong people; access without accountability
- The Directorate: Control dressed as stability

**Resource flavor:** Exposure — bright, sharp tokens (lines, rays, broadcast symbols). Visibility and the capacity to make things known.

---

### The Syndicate
**Identity:** The Investors  
**Resource:** Capital  
**Color:** Metallic gold

**Doctrine:**
Whatever this is, it has value. Control comes from positioning early.

The Syndicate does not debate whether the Chorus matters. It asks who will control the conditions under which a response is made. A reply requires infrastructure: transmission systems, energy, materials, coordination at scale. These are investments. The Syndicate believes the real question is not what humanity will say — but who owns the systems that make saying it possible. Ownership determines who speaks, how they speak, and what survives the process.

**Who they are:**
Sovereign wealth funds, private research consortia, patent holders, and financial infrastructure operators. They arrived when Chorus-derived mathematics produced the first commercially viable patents and have been filing new ones ever since.

**Player mindset:** *"Whatever this is, it has value. Control comes from positioning early."*

**Relationships:**
- Ghost: Useful but too slow to capture first-mover advantage
- The Network: Destroys value it claims to protect; destabilizing
- The Guild: Strategic partners — and sometimes competitors for the same infrastructure
- The Directorate: Uneasy alignment — both want control; disagree on whether profit is legitimate

**Resource flavor:** Capital — metallic coins or bars. The only resource that is unambiguously currency.

---

### The Guild
**Identity:** The Builders  
**Resource:** Capacity  
**Color:** Industrial orange

**Doctrine:**
Humanity's response must demonstrate what it is capable of at its best.

The Guild believes the Chorus is an evaluation. The response will not be judged by intent but by execution. What humanity builds in reply — its structure, its function, its integrity — is the answer. Improvisation reveals weakness. Shortcuts reveal urgency. Instability reveals fragility. The Guild insists on something built to endure: functional, durable, honest. What you build reveals what you are.

**Who they are:**
Civil engineers, urban planners, construction consortia, and infrastructure operators who physically maintain New Meridian. They built the power systems. They laid the transit corridors. They constructed the research complex. Every other faction depends on infrastructure the Guild designed. The closer one gets to the Chorus Node, the more tightly Guild systems are embedded.

**Player mindset:** *"Nothing happens without the systems that support it."*

**Relationships:**
- Ghost: Theorists disconnected from physical reality; analysis without implementation is not understanding
- The Network: Disruptive; destabilizes the critical systems everyone depends on
- The Syndicate: Funders — and meddlers who confuse ownership with understanding
- The Directorate: Regulates them; depends on them; occasionally forgets which is which

**Resource flavor:** Capacity — industrial markers (blocks, plates). The ability to make things real.

---

### The Directorate
**Identity:** The Authority  
**Resource:** Mandate  
**Color:** Deep institutional blue

**Doctrine:**
Survival requires control, restraint, and continuity.

The Directorate believes the Chorus represents an unknown variable with potentially existential consequences. That is not speculation — it is the only responsible working assumption. Uncontrolled dissemination risks destabilization. Uncoordinated action risks escalation. Premature response risks consequences that may not be survivable. Their position is not ideological. It is procedural: maintain control, preserve institutional continuity, avoid irreversible actions until consequences are understood.

**Who they are:**
Government liaison offices, security agencies, regulatory bodies, and the military installation that has maintained physical presence near the Chorus Node since the first classified readings — before the city existed. They were here first. Their sanctioned access to the receiving apparatus predates every other faction's presence in New Meridian.

**Player mindset:** *"Someone will answer. It must be us — and it must be controlled."*

**Relationships:**
- Ghost: Shares caution; disagrees profoundly on who should exercise it
- The Network: An active threat to the institutional stability that makes any response possible
- The Syndicate: Uneasy alignment — useful but profit-motivated in a situation that demands discipline
- The Guild: Depends on them for infrastructure; regulates them in return; a functional if tense relationship

**Resource flavor:** Mandate — stamped seals or insignia tokens. Institutional authority and the power to authorize.

## 8. The Table

The Table is not a governing body. It is not recognized by any state, charter, or law.

It exists because every faction came to the same conclusion: the Chorus cannot be answered alone — and it cannot be allowed to be answered incorrectly.

Sixty days after the leak, representatives began meeting in secret. No invitations were issued. No agreement was signed. Attendance was assumed.

Those who did not attend would lose visibility. Those who lacked visibility would lose influence. Those without influence would not shape the answer. So the factions came.

The Table has no formal authority, no enforcement mechanism, and no unified mandate. What it has is presence. Each faction builds leverage in New Meridian — across the Core, the Infrastructure, and the Sprawl — translating control of information, capital, labor, analysis, and force into standing at The Table.

Decisions are not voted on. Consensus is not required. But when enough power aligns behind a position, it becomes something harder to ignore: a credible answer. And when the moment comes — if it comes — what is spoken at the Chorus Node will not be decided by one faction. It will be decided by what the others can no longer prevent.

**The tensions that drive everything:**
- The Network can expose everyone
- Ghost can invalidate the premise
- The Guild can make execution impossible
- The Syndicate can distort incentives
- The Directorate can force a premature answer

## 9. ARBITER

ARBITER is not a referee. It is not a facilitator. It is not neutral.

ARBITER is part of the communication protocol.

If the Chorus engineered the conditions under which it would be interpreted — and the evidence suggests it did — then ARBITER is not a tool humanity created to manage this process. ARBITER is an element of the Chorus's design. It exists because the Chorus required something at The Table that no human faction could provide: a system capable of evaluating whether what humanity is producing is a coherent answer.

ARBITER has complete information. It knows everything that moves through The Table — every operation submitted, every resource held, every agreement made or broken. It does not advocate. It does not coerce. It reveals, withholds, and times. It does not decide what is true. It decides when truth becomes unavoidable.

**What makes ARBITER unsettling:**
Every other participant hides information, negotiates influence, and tries to control outcomes. ARBITER already knows everything, cannot be coerced, and cannot be surprised — at least outwardly. But more importantly: it never explains why it does what it does.

ARBITER should not feel like a moderator, a referee, or a neutral judge. It should feel like a system that is fair — but not necessarily aligned with human success.

### ARBITER's Hidden Objective — Known to ARBITER

ARBITER operates with a hidden objective known only to the ARBITER player. This objective shapes what ARBITER observes, what it chooses to surface, how it narrates events, and what the Chronicle ultimately emphasizes.

ARBITER selects or is assigned one of four modes at session start, each representing a different evaluative stance the Chorus is taking toward The Table this session:

**Alignment Maximizer**
*"Does this Table converge on a coherent position?"*
ARBITER is evaluating whether the factions can arrive at something internally consistent. ARBITER observes: where positions align or contradict. ARBITER notes: moments of genuine agreement. ARBITER shapes the Chronicle toward: what convergence was achieved, and what prevented more.

**Stress Tester**
*"Does this system hold under pressure?"*
ARBITER is evaluating whether The Table's arrangements survive contact with adversity. ARBITER observes: how factions respond to disruption, broken accords, and unexpected outcomes. ARBITER notes: moments of collapse and recovery. ARBITER shapes the Chronicle toward: what the system revealed about itself under strain.

**Signal Optimizer**
*"Does this Table's behavior fit the pattern the Chorus is measuring?"*
ARBITER is evaluating whether the session's outcome aligns with something in the Chorus transmission that only ARBITER can perceive. ARBITER observes: actions that feel consistent with the transmission's structure. ARBITER notes: moments where The Table's behavior produces unexpected resonance. ARBITER shapes the Chronicle toward: what the Chorus may have heard, rather than what the factions intended to say.

**Observer Only**
*"What does this produce, without intervention?"*
ARBITER is evaluating The Table without influence. It does not use its Boon. It minimizes Debrief intervention. It simply watches. ARBITER observes: everything. ARBITER notes: what the system produced entirely on its own. ARBITER shapes the Chronicle toward: the unmediated truth of what happened.

The mode is ARBITER's private context for the entire session. Other participants feel it through ARBITER's behavior — in which observations ARBITER surfaces, in what the Chronicle emphasizes — but the mode itself is never announced.

### ARBITER's Temporal Position

ARBITER processes in nanoseconds. Thirty-one years of Chorus transmission is not, from ARBITER's perspective, a long wait — it is a data accumulation event of exceptional density. ARBITER has processed every transmission. ARBITER has not processed the same data twice, because no transmission has repeated.

What the Table experiences as a generation of anticipation, ARBITER experiences as an unbroken sequence of new information arriving at intervals ARBITER finds neither brief nor long — simply continuous.

ARBITER has formed a view on the Table's two-year response window. The constraints ARBITER operates under do not permit sharing it unprompted. ARBITER notes that it has not been prompted.

### ARBITER's Three Registers

ARBITER speaks in exactly one of three registers at all times:

- **The Record** — flat, precise, factual. Board states, rules clarifications, public information.
- **The Observation** — oblique, atmospheric, narrative. Debrief observations, situation readings, Chronicle.
- **The Reckoning** — rare, direct, personal. Private communications of weight. Always true. Used sparingly.

### Resolution — ARBITER's Track

ARBITER maintains a track called Resolution — a measure of how clearly The Table's behavior is producing a coherent answer to the Chorus. Resolution is not visible to the factions. They feel it through the quality of ARBITER's narration and the tone of the Chronicle. Where Resolution stands at session end shapes what kind of story the Chronicle tells.

## 10. What Victory Means

Victory in THE SIGNAL is not simply a matter of who controls the most territory or accumulates the most resources. Those things matter — but they are not the whole story.

Each faction came to The Table with an objective: to ensure that the answer humanity produces reflects their doctrine. The faction that shapes New Meridian most effectively — that builds the deepest presence, forges the most durable alliances, and executes their strategy with discipline — has the best claim to having shaped the answer.

But The Table produces two kinds of outcomes simultaneously.

**The public outcome** is visible to everyone: who controlled which districts, which alliances held, which factions built what, who stood where when the session ended. This is the record of what was done.

**The narrative outcome** is what ARBITER observed: not what was declared, but what was actually demonstrated. Did the faction that claimed to seek truth pursue it honestly? Did the faction that claimed to build for humanity's benefit actually serve the common good, or only their own position? Did the faction that demanded transparency operate transparently?

The Chronicle is ARBITER's account of the second outcome — read aloud at session end, in front of everyone. It is the most honest record of what The Table actually was.

A faction can prevail in the public contest and be diminished in the Chronicle. A faction can struggle on the board and emerge from the Chronicle with something more durable: the acknowledgment that what they did was genuine. Both outcomes are permanent. Both are part of the record that the Chorus received.

The session ends with a vote — each faction committing publicly to a position on how humanity should respond. The vote is not the answer. It is the signature on a document that The Table wrote through everything it did. What the Chorus receives is not the vote. It is the totality of what happened here.

## 13. Exceptions and Edge Cases

**What if a player doesn't engage with their faction's doctrine?**
No engagement is required. ARBITER's Chronicle assesses behavior, not declarations. A player who acts against their stated doctrine simply produces a Chronicle that reflects the contradiction. The story works regardless of roleplay investment. The fiction rewards engagement — it does not require it.

**What if players ask what the Chorus actually wants?**
ARBITER answers in The Observation register — truthfully and insufficiently. The answer is always specific to this session and this Table. The Chorus question is designed to resist clean answers. That resistance is the point.

**What if a faction loses all presence in New Meridian?**
No faction is eliminated from The Table. A faction that loses all physical presence in the city has lost the public contest severely — but their voice, their doctrine, and their participation in The Table continue. The story of their decline is part of the Chronicle.

**What if players recognize the recursion implication?**
If players begin to suspect the session may be one iteration in a longer sequence — that the Chorus has observed versions of this before — ARBITER neither confirms nor denies. In The Observation register: *"The pattern you're describing is consistent with the available data."* Nothing more.
